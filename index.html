Aurora SR-91: Unveiling Supersonic & Hypersonic Propulsion with Detonation Engines

Abstract

The paper presents a conceptual design for a high-speed hypersonic aircraft, with a primary focus on the Aurora SR-91, often known as 'the plane that doesn't exist'. This aircraft supposedly uses a Pulse Wave Detonation engine which in turn is more effecient in reaching hyper sonic speeds. The key principle behind a PWDE is the use of detonation, which is a much more efficient form of combustion than the deflagration typically seen in traditional engines. Detonation combustion produces higher pressure and temperature gradients, which can lead to improved engine efficiency and power output.

This paper delves into the specifics of the SR-91's propulsion system, emphasizing its role in the capability of hypersonic flight by also looking at key factors; flow behaviour and impulse. Of particular interest is the propulsion system, a component that significantly impacts aircraft performance. By combining the flow behavior and impulse, in the propulsion system, two important parameters, it represents the cumulative effect of force applied over time can be calculated. Unfortunately, this data is not available for the Aurora SR-91, as this is a highly classified aircraft.

Pulse Wave Detonation Engine

A Pulse Wave Detonation Engine (PWDE) is a type of propulsion technology that aims to achieve efficient and controlled detonation combustion for improved engine performance. The operation of a PWDE involves several distinct phases, including compression, detonation, and expansion. Here's an overview of how a PWDE operates, including the principles of these phases:

Compression Phase: The process begins with the compression phase, similar to conventional engines. Air (or an air-fuel mixture) is drawn into the combustion chamber by the movement of the piston. Unlike traditional engines, a PWDE uses shockwave-based compression, where a reflected shockwave from a previous cycle or a separate source compresses the fresh air in the combustion chamber. This creates a highly compressed and pressurized environment, which is crucial for the subsequent detonation phase

Detonation Phase: In the detonation phase, the fuel and oxidizer (usually air) mixture is ignited by a spark plug or other ignition source. However, the key difference is that the combustion process transitions from deflagration to detonation. In a detonation, the reaction front travels through the mixture at a speed greater than the speed of sound. This rapid combustion generates a shockwave that significantly increases pressure and temperature within the combustion chamber.

Expansion Phase: The expansion phase follows the detonation event.As the combustion wave expands through the combustion chamber, it exerts a tremendous force on the piston. This force causes the piston to move, thereby converting the high-pressure gases into mechanical work. This is where the engine's power is produced. The expansion phase is similar to the power stroke in a traditional internal combustion engine, where the expanding gases push the piston.

The unique operational principles of a Pulse Wave Detonation Engine (PWDE), with its emphasis on controlled detonation combustion, result in distinct flow behavior within the combustion chamber. Understanding this flow behavior is crucial for optimizing engine performance and addressing challenges associated with shockwaves, pressure fluctuations, and heat management.
