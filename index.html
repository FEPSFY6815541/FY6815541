Aurora SR-91: Unveiling Supersonic & Hypersonic Propulsion with Detonation Engines

Abstract

This paper introduces a conceptual design for a high-speed hypersonic aircraft, with a primary focus on the Aurora SR-91, colloquially known as 'the plane that doesn't exist.' The aircraft purportedly utilizes a Pulse Wave Detonation engine, renowned for its efficiency in reaching hypersonic speeds. The PWDE operates on the principle of detonation, a significantly more efficient form of combustion compared to the deflagration observed in traditional engines, resulting in higher pressure and temperature gradients. This paper delves into the specifics of the SR-91's propulsion system, emphasizing its pivotal role in achieving hypersonic flight by examining key factors such as flow behavior and impulse and explaining them in terms of the Ideal-Detonation Theory.

Introduction

Aircraft design is a distinct discipline within aerospace engineering, standing alone amid counterparts like aerodynamics, materials, controls, and propulsion. The Aurora SR-91, with the capability to fly up to 135,000 ft and cruise at Mach 5-6, ventures into the realm of hypersonic flight. To elucidate the Pulse Wave Detonation Engine (PWDE), we employ a theoretical model, simplifying the complex principles that underlie its hypersonic capabilities. The Zeldovich-von Neumann-Doering (ZND) ideal-detonation theory provides a valuable and simplified theoretical framework, facilitating manageable mathematical analysis and insights into impulse generation, thereby contributing to the understanding of high-speed propulsion systems.

Pulse Wave Detonation Engine

A Pulse Wave Detonation Engine (PWDE) is a propulsion technology aiming for efficient and controlled detonation combustion, enhancing engine performance through distinct phases of compression, detonation, and expansion. In the compression phase, the shockwave-based compression sets the stage for subsequent detonation, a process where the fuel and oxidizer mixture transitions from deflagration to detonation, generating a shockwave that significantly increases pressure and temperature. The expansion phase converts the high-pressure gases into mechanical work, akin to the power stroke in traditional engines. The paper explores the theoretical foundations of PWDEs, emphasizing the ideal-detonation theory's role in understanding rapid and efficient combustion processes characterized by shockwaves.

The unique operational principles of a Pulse Wave Detonation Engine (PWDE), with its emphasis on controlled detonation combustion, will be viewed as well from a theoretical point. The ideal-detonation theory often represented by Zeldovich-von Neumann-Doering is a fundamental theoretical framework for understanding the behavior of ideal detonations, which are rapid and efficient combustion processes characterized by shock waves. This theory explains how impulse is generated during the detonation phase and how it contributes to propulsion.Understanding this flow behavior is crucial for optimizing engine performance and addressing challenges associated with shockwaves, pressure fluctuations, and heat management.

Ideal-Detonation Theory

In the Zeldovich–von Neumann–Doering (ZND) model, the reciprocal relationship ρu = constant signifies a dynamic interplay between velocity and density as the detonation wave progresses through the reaction zone. This relationship has profound implications for thrust generation in PWDEs. The initiation of a steady ZND detonation involves an incoming unburnt reactant mixture moving at the supersonic shock velocity (D) (Reference1). As this mixture encounters the inviscid shock, pressure undergoes a discontinuous increase to its maximum value, driven by shock compression. Simultaneously, the velocity behind the shock wave transitions from supersonic to subsonic values. The pressure then gradually decreases to its equilibrium state due to flow expansion through the reaction zone, marking the initial phase of the detonation process. "The temperature within the combustion chamber experiences a twofold change during this phase. Firstly, there's an immediate increase due to shock compression. Subsequently, as the reaction progresses through the reaction zone, temperature continues to rise owing to the heat release associated with the reaction" (Reference1). This intricate interplay between shock compression and heat release is precisely captured by the ZND model. Within the ZND framework, the subsonic velocity in the reaction zone steadily increases to its equilibrium state (V∞ = UCJ), aligning with the local sonic condition, where the velocity of the gas equals the speed of sound. This velocity behavior has a reciprocal impact on density, dictated by the one-dimensional nature of the problem and the constancy of mass flux (ρu = constant) (Reference1).

The increase in subsonic velocity, governed by the ZND model's depiction of the detonation process, translates into a corresponding increase in thrust. As the detonation wave progresses, the high-pressure gases expand, and the force exerted on the engine components is amplified. This force, resulting from the reciprocal relationship between velocity and density, propels the aircraft forward, providing the necessary thrust for propulsion. In essence, the reciprocal relationship expressed by ρu = constant within the ZND framework directly influences the thrust generated by PWDE

Flow Behavior: Impulse

Now, linking this back to the broader understanding of impulse generation in PWDEs, the sudden increase in pressure, as described in the ZND model, becomes the driving force behind the generation of impulse. "This impulse, a result of the dynamic interplay within the ZND detonation process, transforms into thrust as the high-pressure gases expand" (Reference1). The expansion of these gases exerts force on the engine components, propelling the aircraft forward. Thus, the ZND model, with its detailed depiction of the physics underlying detonation, serves as a foundational guide for optimizing PWDEs to harness the generated impulse effectively, ultimately enhancing efficiency and power output.

Conclusion

In summary, within the ZND framework, the interplay between subsonic velocity and density is a manifestation of the conservation laws governing fluid dynamics. The one-dimensional nature of the detonation process, as described by the ZND model, allows us to mathematically express these relationships, providing a deeper understanding of the intricate physics at play within Pulse Wave Detonation Engines.
